\begin{abstract}
Real-time score following from sheet images remains challenging because the model must process streaming audio while resolving highly repetitive visual patterns under strict latency constraints. Recent image-based methods have attempted to use multi-resolution prediction by simultaneously predicting the positions of the active system, bar, and note. However, their predictions across these different levels of notation are independent, which makes the predictions unstable and introduces unnecessary extra search space for bar- and note-level predictions. Most existing methods also lack mechanisms to recover from score discontinuities, such as repeats, D.C., or coda jumps. This paper proposes CODA, to the best of our knowledge, the first real-time score following system that addresses both gaps. CODA explicitly exploits the cascaded structure of music scores: it first selects the active system, then the active bar within it, and finally the active note within the selected bar. This enforces prediction consistency across resolutions. A silence-driven mechanism enables jump recovery whenever a pause is detected, indicating a score discontinuity. CODA's architecture combines a visual encoder with a causal Mamba state-space audio encoder. Candidate positions are scored using audio-conditioned modulation and cross-attention, and decoded using beam search with learned transition penalties. Both training and evaluation are based on the Multimodal Sheet Music Dataset (MSMD) piano benchmarks. CODA achieves strong tracking and discontinuity-recovery accuracy under real-time audio throughput.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Real-time score following is the process of aligning a live performance with the corresponding music score as the music proceeds in real time. It is fundamental for downstream applications, including automatic accompaniment, automatic page-turning, synchronized score display, and many other interactive music systems \cite{schwarz2015score,dannenberg2006music}. Classical approaches use online Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) or probabilistic state models to achieve real-time alignment \cite{dixon2005line,cont2009coupled,nakamura2015real}. However, these methods require a symbolic score such as MIDI or MusicXML, which is not always available.

Image-based score following has recently emerged by working directly on sheet images. Early work classified positions into coarse staff-level buckets on small local image crops \cite{dorfer2016towards}, limiting both spatial resolution and the field of view. Reinforcement-learning methods widened the view to score strips but still operated on narrow windows, making recovery difficult when the agent drifted off track \cite{dorfer2018rl,henkel2019score}. Full-page segmentation addressed the field-of-view limitation by predicting a pixel-level heatmap over the entire page \cite{henkel2020learning}, but only localized at the note level without explicit system or bar tracking. The most recent of these image-based methods proposed multi-resolution detection with You Only Look Once (YOLO)-based object detection frameworks \cite{henkel2021real}. This work predicts the positions of systems, bars, and notes simultaneously using three independent output heads. However, there are no constraints or cascaded structures among these predictions. Nothing enforces that the predicted note lies within the predicted bar, or that the predicted bar lies within the predicted system. The model implicitly learns structural consistency, and disagreements among the three prediction levels can always occur. For example, the predicted note position is in a different bar from the predicted bar position, and the predicted bar position is in a different system from the predicted system. 

This independence causes another problem. Because each head independently predicts over the full page, the bar and note heads must search all candidates on the page, increasing the likelihood of errors and instabilities. A cascaded structure would narrow the search at each stage: once the system is selected, only bars within that system are candidates; once a bar is selected, the candidate note positions are confined to that bar. More fundamentally, this method frames score tracking as an object detection problem. It detects from scratch at every audio frame, predicting bounding boxes as if the system and bar locations were unknown. However, in music performances, the score is static, and all candidate positions are known in advance. Detection is therefore unnecessary. The real task is to select which of the known candidates is currently being played. 

Additionally, existing image-based methods lack mechanisms to recover from score discontinuities such as repeats, D.C., or coda jumps, even though such events are common in practice and have long been addressed in symbolic score following \cite{nakamura2015real,morsi2022bottlenecks,park2025matchmaker}.

This paper proposes CODA, a cascaded online alignment framework that addresses these gaps. The key observation is that on a fixed score page, all system and bar locations are already known. The tracking problem is therefore better formulated as a selection task among known candidates. CODA follows the cascaded structure of music scores directly: it first selects the active system among all systems, then the active bar within that selected system, and finally the note position inside the selected bar. This formulation naturally enforces geometric consistency and narrows the search space at each stage.

CODA combines a convolutional page encoder, whose features are shared across all three cascade stages, with a causal Mamba state-space audio encoder \cite{gu2024mamba}. At both the system and bar stages, visual features from each candidate region are modulated by the audio context via Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) \cite{perez2018film} and refined via cross-attention over the recent audio history. The note stage regresses continuous coordinates within the selected bar. At inference time, rather than committing to a single prediction per frame, CODA maintains multiple candidate paths. Each path is scored by combining model confidence with learned transition penalties that encourage the smooth progression of the model's predicted positions.

To handle score discontinuities, CODA incorporates a silence-driven break mode inspired by the observation of Nakamura et al.\ \cite{nakamura2015real} that real-world score jumps always come after short pauses. When a pause is detected, the tracker suppresses transition penalties and widens the search to cover all system candidates on the page. When audio resumes, CODA relocalizes the active system from the expanded candidate set of systems after the discontinuity. Once the new system has been fetched, normal cascaded tracking can continue. This provides an explicit causal recovery mechanism. 

The major contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
\item We formulate the image-based score as a cascaded selection task over known system and bar candidates.
\item We design a causal streaming architecture that combines Mamba audio encoding, shared page features, audio cross-attention at both the system and bar levels, and beam search with learned transition penalties.
\item We introduce a silence-driven break mode for jump recovery that widens the candidate search during detected pauses and relocalizes when audio resumes.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews symbolic and image-based score following methods as well as prior work on handling repeats and jumps. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes the CODA architecture in detail, including the selection cascade, beam search, training curriculum, jump augmentation, and break mode. Section~\ref{sec:eval} presents the evaluation protocol. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} discusses limitations and future directions.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Symbolic Score Following}
Classical real-time score following relies on symbolic scores such as MIDI or MusicXML. Online DTW \cite{dixon2005line} provides a straightforward baseline by incrementally matching audio frames to score events. Probabilistic state-space models offer richer temporal modeling: Cont \cite{cont2009coupled} introduced a coupled duration-focused architecture, while Arzt and Widmer \cite{aagotnes2011towards} addressed robustness under arbitrary performer deviations using multi-agent tracking. Particle-filter methods such as that of Korzeniowski et al.\ \cite{korzeniowski2013tracking} extended the probabilistic framework to handle tempo changes and rests. Nakamura et al.\ \cite{nakamura2015real} modeled repeats and skips with an explicit transition structure and observed that the majority of real-world score jumps are preceded by short silent breaks. Jiang and Raphael \cite{jiang2020score} proposed a switching state-space model that tracks hidden tempo changes, improving alignment stability under expressive performance variation. More recently, Peter et al.\ \cite{peter2025pairing} combined real-time piano transcription with symbol-level tracking, achieving strong results by leveraging automatic music transcription as a front end. Matchmaker \cite{park2025matchmaker} has systematized evaluation of symbolic methods into an open-source benchmarking framework, though it does not cover image-based trackers.

\subsection{Image-Based Score Following}
Dorfer et al.\ \cite{dorfer2016towards} first showed that a multimodal convolutional neural network (CNN) can follow sheet music without symbolic input, classifying position into discrete staff-level buckets on small image crops. Reinforcement-learning formulations then framed the task as agent navigation over score strips \cite{dorfer2018rl,henkel2019score}. Henkel et al.\ \cite{henkel2020learning} moved to full-page processing via audio-conditioned U-Net segmentation, predicting a pixel mask whose center of mass gives the note position. CYOLO \cite{henkel2021real} extended this to multi-resolution detection: three independent YOLO-style heads predict note, bar, and system bounding boxes from FiLM-conditioned feature maps. However, the three heads predict in parallel without cross-level constraints, each frame is decoded independently of the previous one, and no recovery mechanism exists for when tracking is lost.

\subsection{Handling Repeats and Jumps}
Score discontinuities, including repeats, D.C., and coda jumps, pose a long-standing challenge for score following systems. In the symbolic domain, Nakamura et al.\ \cite{nakamura2015real} handle arbitrary repeats and skips through explicit state transitions. For image-based alignment, Shan and Tsai \cite{shan2020improved} proposed hierarchical DTW to handle repeats and jumps in offline audio-to-sheet-image synchronization without requiring prior knowledge of jump locations. Morsi and Serra \cite{morsi2022bottlenecks} identified repeat handling as one of the key bottlenecks in audio-to-score alignment research. Despite this recognized importance, no existing real-time image-based score following method includes an explicit mechanism for recovering from score discontinuities during online tracking.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

The current score page is assumed to be known and to remain fixed until an external page turn occurs. For each page, layout metadata provides a finite set of systems and bars together with their bounding boxes. The tracker is therefore page-local: it selects among candidate systems and bars on the current page, and it resets its page-local state when the displayed page changes.

At frame $t$, let $h_t=(x_{\le t},\,I)$ denote the audio history and score page image. The model predicts three quantities:
\begin{itemize}
\item the active system index $s_t$,
\item the active bar index $b_t$ within that system,
\item the continuous note position $u_t=(c_x,c_y)$ in bar-local coordinates.
\end{itemize}

This hierarchy is modeled explicitly as
\begin{equation}
p(s_t, b_t, u_t \mid h_t) = p(s_t \mid h_t)\; p(b_t \mid s_t, h_t)\; p(u_t \mid b_t, s_t, h_t).
\end{equation}
This factorization differs from flat detection in two ways. First, it enforces valid geometric structure by construction. Second, it reduces the decision space stage by stage, which is especially helpful in repetitive passages.

\subsection{Audio and Visual Encoders}

The audio stream is converted into a 78-dimensional log-filterbank representation at roughly 20 frames per second. A causal Mamba encoder \cite{gu2024mamba} processes these frames online and produces two outputs: a global conditioning vector $z_t$ and a short sequence of recent hidden states. The global vector is used for FiLM conditioning, while the hidden-state sequence provides a compact audio history for cross-attention.

The score page is processed by a shared convolutional backbone with a feature pyramid network (FPN) head. The backbone takes a grayscale page image scaled to a fixed width and applies FiLM conditioning at the deeper convolutional stages, so that audio context modulates the visual features before they reach the selection heads. The FPN merges multi-scale features into a stride-8 feature map that is shared by all three stages. Because the page image is static within a page, all subsequent decisions operate on these audio-modulated page features together with the streaming audio representation.

\subsection{Hierarchical Selection Cascade}

\subsubsection{System Selection}

The first stage scores all systems on the current page. For each system box, Region of Interest (ROI) Align extracts a local feature map from the shared page features. These ROI features are modulated by FiLM using $z_t$, processed by two convolutional blocks, and then refined by cross-attention over the buffered Mamba hidden states. The head outputs one logit per system, followed by a log-softmax over all page-local system candidates.

\subsubsection{Bar Selection}

After the system is chosen, the bar head scores only the bars inside that system. It follows the same ROI-based design as the system head---FiLM conditioning, two convolutional blocks, cross-attention over the Mamba buffer, pooling, and a scalar score per candidate. Cross-attention is used at both stages because bars within a system can be visually repetitive (e.g., repeated patterns or sequences), and attending to recent audio history helps disambiguate them beyond what modulation alone provides.

\subsubsection{Continuous Note Localization}

The final stage regresses a continuous position inside the selected bar. A note head takes the bar ROI, applies FiLM conditioning, and predicts $(c_x,c_y)\in[0,1]^2$ after a sigmoid layer. These values are converted back to page coordinates using the selected bar box. Continuous regression is used rather than discrete note buckets because it avoids quantization error and preserves smooth within-bar motion.

\subsection{Temporal Priors and Beam Search}

Streaming inference combines the system and bar scores with learnable temporal priors:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{score}(s,b) = \log p(s) + \tau_s(s,s_{t\!-\!1}) + \log p(b \mid s) + \tau_b(b,b_{t\!-\!1}),
\end{equation}
where $\tau_s$ and $\tau_b$ are page-local transition penalties. The priors are learned end to end and bounded to remain non-positive, with stay transitions anchored at zero. This gives the tracker a mild preference for locally smooth motion without hard-coding extreme penalties.

In normal operation, the model scores all systems, keeps the top $k$ systems, then scores bars inside each surviving system and keeps the top $m$ bars per system. The note head is evaluated only on the resulting beam. This makes inference efficient while still allowing the model to compare all systems before pruning.

\subsection{Training}

\subsubsection{Ground-Truth Validation}

Before training, each frame is checked against the page layout metadata. Frames whose system or bar labels cannot be mapped consistently to the current page are marked invalid and removed from the training set. This step is important because silent label fallback can hide broken supervision and distort the temporal-prior chain.

\subsubsection{Two-Phase Curriculum}

Training proceeds in two phases. In Phase 1, the bar and note stages use ground-truth system routing. This lets the model learn clean candidate discrimination. In Phase 2, scheduled sampling gradually replaces ground-truth routing with the model's own system predictions. When the predicted system does not contain the ground-truth bar, the bar and note losses for that sample are masked while the system loss remains active. This exposes the model to its own routing errors and reduces the train--inference gap.

\subsubsection{Losses and Regularization}

System and bar stages are trained with cross-entropy, and the note head with mean-squared error on the bar-local position. The three losses are combined using either fixed task weights or learned uncertainty-based calibration. Data augmentation includes audio padding, tempo perturbation, impulse-response convolution for acoustic variation, and cold-start truncation that randomly shortens the audio context to prevent over-reliance on long histories.

\subsection{Jump Augmentation}

To train recovery behavior, the training pipeline includes on-the-fly jump augmentation. With a controllable probability, a training sample is replaced by audio and targets from another frame in the same piece. The sampled destination may represent a repeat, skip, restart, bar correction, or cross-page jump. For same-page jumps, the previous system and bar labels are frozen at the source frame's current position. This means the destination must overcome a biased temporal prior during training. For cross-page jumps, the priors are reset because page-local indices do not transfer across pages.

The jump window is aligned with the later inference mechanism. Each jump sample contains a short silence gap followed by a brief post-silence segment from the destination. This teaches the model to relocalize from resumed audio after a pause rather than from an unrealistically long clean context.

\subsection{Break Mode for Within-Page Recovery}

The main inference-time recovery mechanism is break mode. Inspired by the silent-break observation of Nakamura et al.\ \cite{nakamura2015real}, the tracker monitors waveform energy with a hysteresis rule. When energy stays low for several consecutive frames, the tracker enters a break state. While in this state, it suppresses transition penalties, widens the system beam to cover all systems on the current page, and freezes the committed tracking state. When audio energy rises again, the tracker keeps transition penalties suppressed for a short grace window, during which it can relocalize and commit a new page-local position. The Mamba state and recent audio buffer are preserved throughout this process.

This mechanism is intentionally page-local. It is meant to recover within-page repeats and skips, while page turns are handled externally by switching the displayed page and resetting the page-local state.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}\label{sec:eval}

The method is evaluated in strict streaming mode on page-aligned piano data from MSMD \cite{dorfer2018learning}. All reported online metrics are produced by causal inference with no future audio. Three metrics are tracked: system accuracy, bar accuracy, and mean page-space localization error in pixels.

Model selection follows the deployment setting rather than validation loss measured with correct-position routing. Each training epoch runs a small streaming validation subset, and every few epochs runs a full validation sweep. The checkpoint with the best streaming bar accuracy is kept as the main model. This criterion is important because losses measured under correct routing can look strong even when online tracking drifts.

Three behaviors are evaluated separately:
\begin{itemize}
\item normal within-page tracking on monotonic validation pieces,
\item robustness under scheduled sampling and beam decoding,
\item recovery on jump-augmented or jump-annotated examples, with and without break mode.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a hierarchical selection framework for real-time score following from sheet images. The core idea is that on a fixed score page, the tracking problem is better solved by selecting among known layout candidates than by detecting from scratch at every frame. This leads to a system--bar--note cascade that enforces geometric consistency by construction, combined with beam-search decoding over learned transition penalties, a training curriculum that gradually exposes the model to its own predictions, jump augmentation, and a silence-driven recovery mechanism for within-page relocalization.

A natural question is whether page-local selection is too restrictive compared to a global model that searches across all pages simultaneously. Page locality suits most practical scenarios: the displayed page is typically known, and the vast majority of tracking decisions are local. Break mode handles the most common within-page discontinuities, while page turns are managed externally. A full multi-page jump model, as in symbolic state-space methods \cite{nakamura2015real}, would extend the framework but is not required for the settings evaluated here.

Several directions remain open. Break mode relies on silence cues, so jumps without pauses (e.g., attacca transitions) are not yet covered. The method also assumes clean layout metadata; robustness to noisy or automatically detected bounding boxes is an important step toward deployment on arbitrary sheet music.
